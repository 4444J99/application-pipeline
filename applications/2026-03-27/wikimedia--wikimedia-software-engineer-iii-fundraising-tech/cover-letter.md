Dear Wikimedia Foundation Hiring Team,

The infrastructure that keeps knowledge free must be as transparent and trustworthy as the knowledge itself. I am a software engineer who builds high-integrity, auditable systems at institutional scale, and I am applying for the Software Engineer III (Fundraising Tech) role because I want to help the Advancement department build the donation infrastructure that sustains one of the most important public goods on the internet. The intersection of technical rigor and mission-driven transparency is not just where I want to work -- it is where my entire engineering practice already lives.

My current system, ORGANVM, is a 113-repository creative infrastructure spanning 8 GitHub organizations with 23,470 tests and 104 CI/CD pipelines. What makes it relevant to Wikimedia is not the raw scale but the governance architecture underneath it. Every repository in my system operates under an auditable promotion state machine -- LOCAL to CANDIDATE to PUBLIC to GRADUATED -- where every state transition is gated by automated validation, every dependency is verified before promotion, and every action is logged for downstream traceability. I built this because I believe that systems handling consequential decisions must be demonstrably correct, not just functionally adequate. In a fundraising context where donor trust depends on absolute transparency and every dollar must be accounted for through every step of the pipeline, this "audit-first" engineering philosophy is not optional. It is the foundation upon which sustainable fundraising infrastructure must be built.

My experience with data integrity and API integrations maps directly to the challenges of the Fundraising Tech stack. I built a 37-script Python CLI suite with 700 tests that integrates with Greenhouse, Ashby, and other external platforms through their APIs, handling complex state transitions, YAML schema validation, signal validation gates, and referential integrity checks across interconnected data sources. I designed these systems to be self-healing because I operate without a platform team or SRE rotation, which means every pipeline must detect its own inconsistencies and every quality gate must run without human intervention. This constraint has made me deeply attuned to the failure modes that emerge when disparate data sources feed into critical workflows -- exactly the challenge of coordinating donation processing across payment providers, CRM systems, and localized fundraising campaigns that must work correctly for communities in dozens of countries.

I also bring a genuine commitment to accessibility and global reach that goes beyond technical implementation. My Omni-Dromenon Engine is an open-source real-time participation platform designed for 1,000+ concurrent users with Docker, Redis, and Nginx deployment, built from the ground up for global accessibility. I documented my entire 113-repository system in 42 published essays totaling approximately 142,000 words, each written with the explicit goal of making complex technical architecture navigable by someone without my context or assumptions. For a role that involves building and maintaining fundraising tools used by Wikimedia communities worldwide, this instinct for making systems accessible across technical, linguistic, and cultural boundaries is as critical as the code that implements them.

Eleven years of teaching 2,000+ students across 8 institutions shaped my engineering practice in ways that pure industry experience cannot replicate. I write code that explains itself because I have spent a decade learning how to explain complex ideas to people who start from fundamentally different places. I build systems that document their own state and surface their own inconsistencies because I know that comprehension, not just functionality, is what makes infrastructure trustworthy over time. I am eager to bring this discipline to the Wikimedia Foundation and help ensure that the systems sustaining free knowledge are as reliable and transparent as the mission they serve.
