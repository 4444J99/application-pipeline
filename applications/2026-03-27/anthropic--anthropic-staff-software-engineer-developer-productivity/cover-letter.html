Before I wrote a line of production code, I spent eleven years teaching composition at eight colleges — standing in front of rooms where twenty-five people were all stuck in different ways, and my job was to figure out which friction was blocking each one before they gave up and disengaged. That is developer productivity work. The domain changed; the diagnostic instinct did not. When I watch someone struggle with a tool, I am not watching for what they are doing wrong. I am watching for where the tool's mental model diverges from theirs.

Anthropic's developer tooling shapes how thousands of engineers interact with the most capable models in the world. That is a leverage point I take seriously — not as an abstraction, but because I have felt both sides of the friction equation. As an architect running a system spanning eight organizations with automated quality gates on every push, I am a power user of exactly the kind of CLI-and-SDK infrastructure this role builds. I have filed the bugs. I have written the workarounds. I have felt the specific frustration of knowing what I want to express to an AI system and having the tooling be the bottleneck between intent and execution. The cover letter you are reading was composed by a human who uses Claude Code daily, in anger, on real production tasks — and who has strong opinions about where the seams show.

What I learned in the classroom was that comprehension failures are almost never about intelligence. They are about sequence — the order in which concepts are introduced, the assumptions baked into each step, the gap between what the instructor thinks they explained and what the student actually received. Developer experience has the same structure. A CLI that requires three flags to do the common case and one flag for the edge case has its priorities inverted. An error message that tells you what went wrong but not what to do next is a lesson plan that ends at the diagnosis and skips the remediation. I have designed assessment rubrics for writing courses and scoring rubrics for software pipelines, and the design principles are identical: make the successful path legible, make failure informative, make iteration cheap.

I have built my own tooling infrastructure out of necessity — a suite of interoperating command-line tools where each script does one thing, reads from a shared data layer, and can be composed into larger workflows. That architecture was not chosen for elegance. It was chosen because when you are the only engineer, you cannot afford tools that require context-switching to understand. Every decision — naming conventions, output formatting, flag defaults, error handling — was tested against the question: will I understand what this does six months from now at 2 AM? That discipline produces different software than what emerges from a team where someone else can always explain the tool. It produces software that must explain itself.

The reason I want to build developer productivity tooling at Anthropic specifically is that the stakes are different here. The gap between a developer who uses Claude effectively and one who bounces off it is not just a product metric — it determines whether the most transformative technology of this decade gets adopted by the people who could do the most good with it. Removing friction from that path is work I would do for free and have done for free. The chance to do it at the source, where every improvement compounds across every developer who touches the SDK, is not an opportunity I can walk past.
