Quality and release engineering at scale is a loneliness problem. Not an emotional one -- a structural one. When the system works, nobody notices. When it breaks at 2 AM, the person who built it is the only one who understands why. dbt Labs needs someone for this role who has felt that weight and built through it, because the Quality & Release Platform does not get built by someone who has only ever had a team behind them.

I spent six years building quality infrastructure as the sole operator of a system that grew until it demanded the discipline of an organization. No one was coming to fix the flaky test. No one was going to refactor the deployment pipeline while I shipped features. Every piece of tooling had to be self-service because I was both the platform team and the only customer. That constraint shaped everything: the promotion gates had to be automated because there was no human reviewer to catch regressions. The CI templates had to be reusable because duplicating workflows across organizations was a maintenance death sentence. The verification matrix had to enforce its own coverage because I could not manually audit every module. The system had to heal itself or it would not survive.

What came out of that constraint is exactly what this role asks for: release infrastructure that teams adopt because it removes friction rather than adding process. My quality ratchets are one-directional -- once a threshold is met, it cannot regress, and no human has to police it. My state machine enforces forward-only transitions with validation at every gate, not because I distrust engineers but because trust does not scale without automation. I built developer CLIs that score, validate, and ship because the alternative was tribal knowledge living in my head. This is the philosophy dbt needs: infrastructure so good that quality becomes the path of least resistance.

The gap between maintaining quality for yourself and maintaining it for hundreds of engineers is not technical -- it is empathetic. You have to care more about the developer who will curse your pipeline at midnight than about the elegance of your architecture. Eleven years of teaching taught me that: the system exists for the person using it, not the person who built it. I want to bring that instinct to dbt Labs.
