Developer evangelism is teaching. Not the conference-circuit, slide-deck, thought-leader kind -- the real kind, where you watch someone's face and know the exact moment they stopped understanding, and you find a different way in. Twilio was built on that insight: the API is the product, and the developer experience is the go-to-market. The evangelist is the person who closes the gap between documentation and comprehension, between "I read the quickstart" and "I shipped the thing."

I have been closing that gap for eleven years, first in classrooms and now through code. Teaching composition to two thousand students across eight institutions taught me that explanation is a design problem: the same concept presented three different ways reaches three different minds, and you do not get to choose which one walks through your door. That instinct now lives in everything I build. My open-source projects ship with onboarding documentation measured in hundreds of thousands of words -- not because I enjoy writing docs, but because I learned that the distance between "interesting project" and "project I will actually use" is almost always a documentation failure. Every repository includes a structured navigation protocol because I know that a developer who cannot find the entry point in sixty seconds will close the tab.

What I bring to Twilio that most evangelists do not is the engineering depth to build the demos that matter. I do not just talk about multi-agent orchestration or API integration -- I have built production systems that submit through ATS APIs, coordinate autonomous agents across eighteen execution phases, and fuse data from network graphs, market feeds, and outreach logs into real-time scoring engines. My MFA gives me narrative craft that makes technical content land with non-technical audiences, and my engineering practice means the code behind the narrative actually runs. That combination -- teacher who ships, engineer who writes -- is rare, and it is exactly what developer evangelism demands.

Twilio changed how developers think about communication APIs. I want to help the next generation of builders discover what is possible with the platform, the same way a good teacher helps a student discover what is possible in themselves.
