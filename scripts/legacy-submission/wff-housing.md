## Artist Statement

**~300 words — copy between the lines:**

---

Building creative infrastructure from lived experience of precarity.

My practice centers on the eight-organ system: a documented infrastructure coordinating theory, generative art, commercial products, governance, public process, community, and marketing across 113 repositories in 8 GitHub organizations. I don't produce individual artworks. I produce the *systems* that generate, coordinate, and sustain creative work — and the visible record of how those systems are built is itself the primary creative output.

The process of creation is the product. The 48 published essays aren't reflections on the work; they ARE the work — the creative process rendered into prose in real time. The governance rules — registry design, dependency graphs, promotion pipelines — aren't bureaucratic overhead; they're generative constraints, the way a composer's harmonic rules shape what melodies can emerge. The system runs 4,000+ automated tests across 104 CI workflows, enforces 50 dependency edges with zero violations, and is grounded in 27 constitutional specs backed by 130 peer-reviewed academic sources. A stakeholder portal provides live public access to the system's health and governance state.

I work in the tradition of solo producers who became orchestras: Brian Eno treating the studio as a compositional instrument, Trent Reznor building entire albums alone because nobody else would commit at the necessary intensity. I use AI tools the way these practitioners used their technologies — as instruments for realizing an architectural vision, not as replacements for creative judgment.

The eight-organ system is simultaneously: (a) working infrastructure that produces creative work, (b) a documented methodology for solo production at institutional scale, (c) an argument that the process of creation — made visible and governable — is itself the most interesting thing an artist can produce, and (d) a reusable model other practitioners can learn from.

## Project Description / Why This Opportunity

---

The eight-organ system grows from lived experience of precarity — housing instability, recovery, the specific vulnerability of building a creative practice without institutional support. ORGAN-VI (Community) and ORGAN-V (Public Process) exist because creative infrastructure should be visible, shared, and governed by the people it serves. 48 published essays document this practice transparently, including the failures.

WFF Housing Stability Grant represents a critical opportunity to sustain and extend this practice. The work is built, documented, and operational — what it needs now is the time and resources to deepen, not to start over.

## Housing Precarity Narrative

I am 35 years old, living in my mother's home in New York as a licensee — no lease, no legal tenancy protections. This is not a lifestyle choice. It is the residual consequence of building a creative practice without inherited wealth, institutional backing, or a partner's second income, compounded by years in adjunct teaching at rates that never covered New York rents.

The material constraint is specific and measurable. Housing precarity imposes a daily cognitive tax that competes directly with sustained creative production. Every week spent navigating the vulnerability of living without stable tenancy — the awareness that this arrangement could end at any time, for any reason — is a week where creative energy is diverted into survival logistics rather than the work itself. Studio practice requires a baseline of spatial and psychological stability that precarious housing undermines at the root.

What $30,000 over three years would change is concrete. It would enable me to secure stable housing in New York City, establish a dedicated workspace, and remove the daily overhead of precarity from the production equation. The eight-organ system is already built and operational — 113 repositories, 4,000+ automated tests, 104 CI workflows, 48 published essays, a 32-day zero-incident soak test, a live stakeholder portal. The infrastructure exists. What it needs is the material foundation that allows its creator to sustain the intensity of production that built it.

This grant would not fund the beginning of a practice. It would fund the removal of the single largest obstacle to a practice that is already producing at institutional scale from the most constrained starting position possible.
