## Post Text

---

The hardest engineering problem
isn't building the system.
It's making the system legible
to someone who didn't build it.

I spent eleven years discovering that — BUT not in a codebase.
In classrooms. 100+ courses. 2,000+ students.
8 institutions. Teaching composition, digital media,
technical communication.¹

Every semester I designed a curriculum
where each concept required the previous one.
Students couldn't skip Module 4 because Module 5
depended on it — THEREFORE the structure
had to be rigorous, testable, and fail-safe.
A curriculum is a dependency graph.²

The MFA in Creative Writing taught me something different:
narrative structure isn't decoration.
It's the architecture that determines
whether an argument lands or collapses.
Parker and Stone's writers' room rule —
every beat connected by "but" or "therefore,"
never "and then" — applies to documentation
the same way it applies to screenwriting.³

THEREFORE when I started building software infrastructure,
I wasn't starting from zero. I was translating
a decade of documentation architecture
into a different medium. The promotion state machine
that governs 113 repositories today
enforces the same maturity progression
I used in curriculum design:
draft → reviewed → published → maintained.

739,000 words of technical documentation.
23,470 automated tests. All built by one person — BUT
the real metric is: could someone else maintain it?
That's the question every teacher answers
every time they hand a syllabus to a stranger.
The best system is the one the next person can own.
