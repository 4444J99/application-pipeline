## Post Text

Before I wrote a single line of production code, I taught 100+ courses to 2,000+ students across 8 institutions.

That background isn't a detour — it's the foundation.

Teaching forces you to:
→ Explain complex systems to people who didn't build them
→ Write documentation that anticipates confusion, not just describes functionality
→ Build error messages that tell you what to DO, not just what broke
→ Design curricula where each concept builds on the last — the same structure as a good codebase

When I started building software infrastructure, I realized I'd already been doing documentation architecture for a decade. The MFA in Creative Writing taught me narrative structure. Teaching taught me audience awareness. Both taught me that the bridge between "I built this" and "someone else can use this" is the hardest engineering problem.

Today I maintain 739,000 words of technical documentation across 113 repositories. The promotion state machine that governs those repos enforces the same maturity progression I used in curriculum design: draft → reviewed → published → maintained.

Teaching 2,000 students taught me that the best system is the one the next person can own.
