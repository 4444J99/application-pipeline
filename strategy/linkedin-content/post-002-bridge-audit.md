## Post Text

---

The bridge between humanities and engineering isn't a gap — it's a discipline.

Last week I posted my credentials. This week I formalized the rules that post violated.

Aristotle's *Poetics* defines recognition as the structural mechanism that makes a scene land — the audience discovers something that was true all along.¹ Trey Parker's writers' room rule kills sequential plotting: if the word "and then" connects your beats instead of "but" or "therefore," the structure has failed.² Phoebe Waller-Bridge requires at least three things happening simultaneously in every scene — argument, credibility, and felt truth layered together, never one at a time.³

I translated fourteen narrative theorists into ninety-two formal algorithms. Then I turned those algorithms into an audit system and pointed it at my own first post.

It scored an average of 1.0 out of 10 across eleven diagnostic articles. Zero on causation — every sentence followed the last without causing it. Zero on collision geometry — three projects listed in parallel, never converging. The only sentence that carried a thesis was buried at the bottom: "The bridge between humanities and engineering isn't a gap — it's a discipline."

That sentence should have opened the post. It should have been the premise the evidence served, not a throwaway closer after a list of numbers.

The same formal rules that tell you whether a scene works tell you whether a LinkedIn post works, whether documentation is navigable, whether a state machine's transition logic is complete. That's not an analogy. When you formalize both into mathematics, they produce identical structural constraints — multiplicative, not additive, collapsing entirely when any single dimension reaches zero.

The bridge is the formalization itself.
