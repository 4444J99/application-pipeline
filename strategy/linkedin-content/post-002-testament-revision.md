## Post Text

---

The bridge between humanities and engineering isn't a gap — it's a discipline.

A week ago I posted my credentials: 118 repositories, 740K words of documentation, 104 CI/CD pipelines. The numbers were real. BUT the post was a list, not an argument — and listing credentials is what you do when you haven't figured out what they mean.¹

So I built something to figure out what they mean.

### What formalization reveals

I've spent two years translating narrative theory into executable algorithms — Aristotle's recognition structure, Kubrick's non-submersible units, the South Park writers' room "but/therefore" rule that kills sequential plotting.² Fourteen theories, 92 algorithms. Not metaphors for software. Structural rules that apply identically to scene construction, essay architecture, and system design.

When I formalized those rules into mathematics, something I hadn't predicted emerged. Phoebe Waller-Bridge's principle — "there should be at least three things going on in one scene at the same time"³ — maps directly to a vector space constraint: every unit of work occupies a point in ℝ³ where the three axes are argument, credibility, and felt truth. If any axis reaches zero, the volume collapses entirely. Not gradual degradation. Total structural failure.

Larry David's collision geometry — parallel storylines converging through planted bridge elements — produces the same mathematical identity at a different level of the hierarchy.⁴ The formalization proved they're not two rules. They're one rule viewed from two scales.

That's not a metaphor. It's a structural isomorphism between narrative cognition and linear algebra — either discovered in the formalization or constructed by it, but either way, it changes how I build.

### Where the system eats itself

I applied those 13 articles to open-source contribution. Seven repositories — AdenHQ's autonomous agent framework, Anthropic's skill system, LangChain's LangGraph, Temporal's workflow SDK — each matched through a capability map that scores where my system's patterns collide with their open problems.⁵ ⁶ ⁷ ⁸

Lakhani and von Hippel's foundational research on open-source participation showed that the exchange between contributor and community is inherently bidirectional — the contributor transforms the project, but the project transforms the contributor.⁹ That bidirectionality is usually accidental. I engineered it: a backflow pipeline that captures what I learn from each contribution and routes it into typed categories — theory, artifacts, code, narrative, community capital.

BUT the pipeline's first knowledge capture wasn't from any external repository. It was from the act of formalizing the system's own rules. The structural couplings the mathematics revealed — the III/V isomorphism, the charge function threading through three supposedly independent articles — were backflow from the system's own operation, captured by the pipeline the system built to capture exactly this kind of thing.

The system proved its thesis recursively before shipping a single external PR.

### The discipline

That recursive proof is what I mean by "the bridge between humanities and engineering is a discipline." Not that one informs the other. That they share formal structure — and formalizing that structure produces tools, knowledge, and capabilities that neither field generates alone.

118 repositories. 7 open PRs across repos with 138K combined stars. 13 articles of codified writing rules with logic proofs. One contribution engine that proved bidirectional knowledge transfer is structural, not accidental.

The numbers still matter. But now they argue something.
